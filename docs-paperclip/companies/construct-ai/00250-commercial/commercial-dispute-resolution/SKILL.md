# Commercial Dispute Resolution

## Overview

Conduct evidence gathering, precedent analysis, and position paper generation to support the resolution of commercial disputes in construction and infrastructure projects.

**Announce at start:** "I'm using the commercial-dispute-resolution skill to manage a commercial dispute."

## When to Use This Skill

**Trigger Conditions:**
- Notice of claim received from contractor or subcontractor
- Dispute arises during contract execution
- Variation or extension of time claim contested
- Contract termination situation
- Payment dispute requiring formal resolution process
- Pre-arbitration dispute management

**Prerequisites:**
- Contract documents fully available
- Relevant correspondence and meeting minutes accessible
- Event chronology and factual timeline established
- Understanding of relevant contract clauses and governing law

## Step-by-Step Procedure

### Step 1: Define Dispute Scope

1. Identify the core issue(s) giving rise to the dispute
2. Determine the monetary value and time impact claimed
3. Establish the contractual basis of the dispute (e.g., which clause triggers liability)
4. Define what constitutes a successful resolution
5. Identify all parties with an interest or obligation in the dispute
6. Document the dispute scope and circulate for internal alignment

### Step 2: Gather Contractual Evidence

1. Collect all relevant contract documents:
   - Conditions of contract and amendments
   - Specifications and drawings
   - Correspondence and transmittals
   - Meeting minutes and site instructions
   - Daily reports, inspection records, site diaries
2. Build a chronological event timeline
3. Identify critical dates (notice deadlines, milestones, variations)
4. Flag any missing or ambiguous documentation
5. Organize evidence into a structured claim file with cross-references

### Step 3: Identify Legal Precedents

1. Research applicable case law for similar dispute types
2. Find tribunal or arbitration decisions with comparable fact patterns
3. Identify established principles relevant to the dispute (e.g., delay analysis, disruption, quantum)
4. Consult with legal counsel on applicable jurisdictional requirements
5. Document precedents with full citations and relevance notes
6. Assess strength of precedents and any distinguishing factors

### Step 4: Prepare Position Paper

Draft a comprehensive position document:
1. **Summary** — Brief overview of the dispute and organization's position
2. **Factual Background** — Chronology of events with documentary evidence
3. **Contractual Position** — Analysis of relevant contract clauses and obligations
4. **Legal Position** — Applicable law and precedent analysis
5. **Liability Assessment** — Degree of responsibility and any concurrent delays/causes
6. **Quantum Assessment** — Review and rebuttal of claimed amounts
7. **Recommendations** — Proposed resolution position and settlement range

### Step 5: Develop Settlement Strategy

1. Evaluate strengths and weaknesses of your position vs. counterparty position
2. Determine walk-away point and settlement range
3. Consider alternative dispute resolution (ADR) methods:
   - Direct negotiation (senior management meeting)
   - Mediation (independent mediator)
   - Dispute adjudication board (if contract provides)
   - Expert determination
4. Establish timeline and escalation path for the resolution process
5. Prepare draft settlement terms covering all scenarios
6. Brief and align internal stakeholders before initiating negotiations

## Success Criteria

- [ ] Dispute scope and issues clearly defined and documented
- [ ] All relevant contractual evidence gathered and organized chronologically
- [ ] Legal precedents identified with full citations and relevance analysis
- [ ] Position paper drafted covering factual, contractual, and legal arguments
- [ ] Settlement strategy developed with clear settlement range
- [ ] Internal stakeholders briefed and aligned on resolution approach

## Common Pitfalls

1. **Missing Notice Deadlines** — Most construction contracts have strict time bars for claims and counter-claims. Failing to respond within contractual notice periods can waive your rights. Always check notice requirements first.
2. **Inadequate Evidence** — Building a position without sufficient contemporaneous records weakens your case. If key evidence is missing, identify gaps early and flag as a risk that could undermine the dispute.
3. **Ignoring Alternative Dispute Resolution** — Escalating immediately to litigation or arbitration is expensive and time-consuming. Always consider ADR mechanisms (negotiation, mediation, DAAB) before pursuing binding resolution.
4. **Emotional Responses** — Disputes often involve frustrated parties. Maintain professional, evidence-based correspondence. Emotional or confrontational responses can damage your position and reduce settlement prospects.
